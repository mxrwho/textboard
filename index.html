I was born a female.

I was born a female, and thus declared “girl”.

Of course, being a “girl” came with its own set of desired characteristics: For pre-puberty those would be “cuteness” and “mildness”.

I was destined to fail in both.

[...]

At my parents’ dinner parties along with the dolls that came as gifts I was given questions: “How many children are you going to have when you grow up?”, “Who are you going to marry?”.

[...]

But when I wasn’t creating worlds of adventure or sorrow in my room, I played outside:

I can’t forgive the class bias for making “dirty” such a negative word. There, in the dirt, I played hide and seek, tag, dodgeball, but most of all I played football with the boys, I fought with the boys, I explored empty buildings and climbed fig trees with the boys. In my mind I was one of them, whatever that was.

I was proclaimed a “tomboy”.

I was strong-willed, resolute, talkative and loud. On good days those characteristics would make me a “leader” but on bad days and since all I could actually lead was my shiny army of marbles, they just made me “stubborn”.

And since adjectives have their own clusters and spectrums, and despite its seeming innocence, “stubborn” often came along with “controversial”, “naysayer” and “quarrelsome”.

And being a “stubborn_tomboy” has an expiration date.

[…]

I got my first period when I was ten. At the time such an event was explicitly phrased as “becoming a woman”.

I was corrected because I walked “like a boy” and was strongly urged to take ballet classes in order to become “graceful”.

My changing body became a source of shame and a hindrance, instead of a source of pride and joy.

As girls grew, expectations extended to being “modest”, “prudent” and “quiet”. Hanging out with the boys, and especially with the “bad” boys, was enough to set off a general alarm and to label me “wild”.

[...]

Eventually I understood that for my friends too I was a “girl”.

I was “different” and the defenses I built during my teenage years were meant to make me “tough” and “strong”.

I was morphing into something “weird” and everything that made “strange” a good thing filled me with comfort and, along with the “misfits” who accepted me, offered a sense of community and belonging.

And although the “wholeness” of a core self is a construct and nothing in us remains unmovable

I had to make a new self, with new vocabulary:

A frame

      “worthy”

a house

        “beautiful”

a room

       “warm”

where I could feel

              “capable”

comfortable and safe

         to exist

allowed to be and to become

            “lovable”

and try and fail and try.
